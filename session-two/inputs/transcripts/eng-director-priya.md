# Stakeholder interview — Priya Raman

- **Speaker:** Priya Raman, Senior Engineering Director
- **Role at Meridian:** Representing engineering leadership at the Summit
- **Interviewer:** Steph Schofield (program team)
- **Date:** March 25, 2026
- **Format:** 30-minute video call, transcribed
- **Note:** Speaker turns only. Light cleanup for readability. Priya
  asked us to flag that she was deliberately blunt — "you asked for the
  engineering perspective; I am giving it to you."

---

**Priya:** Thank you for asking me. I'll be direct because that's more
useful than polite. The Summit, in the shape it's heading, is going to
under-serve the people who actually build things at Meridian. I want
to flag that now while there's still time to fix it.

**Priya:** Here's what I keep saying and what I'll say again on record:
developer experience is a strategic investment for this company, not a
comfort item. Our cloud bills are partly high because engineers wait
six minutes for a CI run and ten more figuring out which test failed.
Some customer-facing teams are slow partly because their internal tools
were built in a hurry three years ago and nobody's been allowed to
invest since. DX shows up in cloud cost, customer responsiveness,
retention of senior engineers, and shipping speed. It is not, despite
what Jane and Marcus apparently believe, an inward-facing topic.

**Priya:** I've heard, more than once, that DX would "bore the
audience." I disagree, and that reveals an assumption about who is in
the audience. The Summit audience is largely people who *use* internal
platforms every day. They will not be bored by a credible discussion of
why those platforms either help or get in their way. What would bore
them is talking about DX in abstract, framework-pitching terms — a
presentation problem, not a topic problem.

**Priya:** I'll also say this directly because it needs saying: the
Summit should not be shaped to make Meridian look good externally.
That's a marketing program, not a Summit program. Optimize the agenda
for press coverage and the most visible sessions become the least
useful internally, and the people in the room will feel patronized.
I'd rather skip the brand angle and have a Summit that genuinely earns
word of mouth over the next twelve months.

**Priya:** AI in workflows — fine as a direction. The submissions are
good. The risk is we end up with thirty variations of the same talk.
Be aggressive about variety: real workflows, different roles, honest
about what didn't work. If every AI session is "and then we shipped it
and it was great," the audience tunes out by lunch.

**Priya:** Cloud spending — important, but at least one cloud session
should be honest about the part where we haven't fully figured it out.
The audience can smell a curated success story from the back of the
room.

**Priya:** Customer stories — fine, important, no notes.

**Priya:** Quantum computing came up in some kickoff conversation.
Please don't. We don't do quantum work. We have no expertise in it.
Inviting an external person to do a quantum talk because someone thought
it would look good is exactly the "performative" thing Marcus warned
about, and I'll be slightly hostile about it if it appears on the
agenda.

**Priya:** On tracks. Jane wants three deep tracks; Marcus wants six
narrower ones. I'm with Marcus. The submission base supports six, and
three force us to lump things that don't naturally lump. The "we honor
the submissions" argument matters more than the "fewer things to
remember" argument here — the audience is technical enough to handle
parallel tracks.

**Priya:** I'll be back when you present the program options. If DX is
not one of them, I'm going to ask why on record. Better to have that
conversation in this transcript than as a surprise in front of broader
leadership.

**End of recording.**
