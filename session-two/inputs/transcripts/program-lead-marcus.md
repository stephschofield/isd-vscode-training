# Stakeholder interview — Marcus Hale

- **Speaker:** Marcus Hale, Director, Program Management Office
- **Role at Meridian:** Operational owner of the Innovation Summit
- **Interviewer:** Steph Schofield (program team)
- **Date:** March 18, 2026
- **Format:** 45-minute video call, transcribed
- **Note:** Speaker turns only. Light cleanup for readability.

---

**Marcus:** I'm the operational guy here, which means I'll be less
inspirational than Jane was. Somebody has to. What I'm watching for is
whether the Summit lands as a *coherent program* or a string of talks
on the same week. Audiences remember structure long after they forget
any individual session.

**Marcus:** Submission volume is up. Just under two hundred in the
cleaned dataset, which is good — it means selection pressure. The risk
at that volume is a program that feels like a buffet. So my first ask:
pick a small number of program directions, name each one, and ruthlessly
sort submissions into them. Anything that doesn't fit gets cut, or you
create a new direction for it deliberately. Don't let random good talks
pull the program out of shape.

**Marcus:** AI in everyday workflows is real. We have forty-eight
submissions on it, more than any other category. Most are good, several
excellent. You can build a strong direction around that with plenty of
selection.

**Marcus:** Cloud spending — also real. We have a solid batch of FinOps
and cost-discipline submissions. It maps to what Jane wants. The risk:
it feels boring next to AI, so be careful about adjacency in the
schedule.

**Marcus:** Customer stories. I want a *full track*, not a sprinkle.
The submissions are there. The challenge is getting field folks
comfortable on stage. Cap at six or seven and make every one excellent
rather than fill ten and water it down.

**Marcus:** On structure — Jane wants three big themes; I want six
smaller tracks. With three, attendees pick one and live in it all day.
With six they get more variety and we honor more of the submission base.
We have the rooms and the slots. Six is *easier* for me, not harder,
because I don't force-fit talks into themes that don't want them. Put
both in front of leadership; I'm recommending six.

**Marcus:** Flagging something Priya raised at the offsite. She wants
developer experience as its own program direction — not just "tooling"
but a strategic frame around DX as an investment that compounds. About
thirty submissions on it, enough for a track. Jane's lukewarm because
it sounds inward-facing. She's half right. There's a version that *is*
inward-facing and would bore everyone, and a version about how we ship
faster and more reliably — the "we run our own platform like adults"
story Jane says she wants. Framed right, it serves both. I'd push for
it.

**Marcus:** Honest call on the kickoff notes: someone — I think Henrik —
floated a quantum computing session. Came up twice. Zero submissions on
it, no in-house speaker who could credibly do it. I would not include
it. If anyone pushes back I'll show them the dataset.

**Marcus:** The external brand angle Jane mentions — I get it, but be
careful. Orient the Summit around brand outcomes and the program will
feel performative. Better to make a Summit useful to the people in the
room and let the brand benefit follow. If we want a "for the cameras"
element, record a few sessions and distribute them after. Don't bend
the program shape around it.

**Marcus:** What I do *not* want to ship is a program where attendees
can't tell why a session was included. Every session traces to a stated
direction with stated criteria, written down before we started picking.
That's the operational ask. The strategy shape is yours and Jane's
call, but the discipline of mapping submissions to directions — that
one's mine, and I'm going to be annoying about it.
